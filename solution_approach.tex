\section{Executive Summary}
Money laundering via retail bank accounts (money mules) breaks transactional audit trails, rendering traditional rule-based banking systems ineffective. While complex machine learning (ML) classifiers can detect these anomalies, regulatory safety guidelines (such as the Federal Reserve Board's SR 11-7 model governance rules) require full interpretability before placing holds on customer accounts.

The \textbf{MuleTrace (MTX)} platform resolves this conflict. It is a real-time, explainable AML decision engine combining a dual-engine (XGBoost and LightGBM) bimodal classifier, an interactive policy scorecard sandbox for audit overrides, and a Local Feature Attribution (LFA) natural language explanation compiler that automatically populates Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) templates.

\section{Data Leakage \& Preprocessing Resolutions}
During baseline data audits, we identified and mitigated two critical data leakages:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Index/Sorting Leakage:} The raw training CSV was pre-sorted by the target variable $Y$ (mule/clean status). Standard decision trees split on row index as a proxy feature, yielding an artificial validation accuracy of $99.9\%$. We dropped row indexes and randomly shuffled the records before training.
    \item \textbf{Temporal Batch Leakage:} Features $F2230$ and $F3912$ acted as exact temporal separators (clean and mule cases were clustered in different months). We excluded both features entirely to force the classifiers to learn structural banking patterns rather than temporal drift.
\end{itemize}

\section{Machine Learning Model Pipeline \& Results}

\subsection{Bimodal Design and Hyperparameters}
MTX rejects a single-model approach in favor of a bimodal pipeline to navigate the trade-offs of financial compliance. Anti-money laundering (AML) operations present asymmetric costs: false positives (freezing clean accounts) cause customer friction and regulatory issues, while false negatives (missed money mules) lead to severe compliance penalties and money laundering. We deploy two models optimized for different operational contexts.

A natural question arises: \textit{if both models are trained with the identical minority class weight penalization (\texttt{scale\_pos\_weight = 110.8}) to handle class imbalance ($0.89\%$), what differentiates their precision-recall trade-offs?} The bimodal differentiation is driven by two distinct mechanisms:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Tree Growth Algorithms and Regularization (Structural Asymmetry):}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{XGBoost (Precision-Focused):} Utilizes a level-wise (depth-wise) tree growth algorithm. Level-wise growth splits all nodes at a given depth simultaneously, enforcing a highly regularized, symmetrical structure. Combined with a rigid maximum depth limit ($\text{depth} = 5$), this structure restricts tree complexity, preventing the model from fitting high-variance anomalies. The regularized structure makes it highly conservative, minimizing false-positive alerts on clean accounts.
        \item \textbf{LightGBM (Recall-Focused):} Utilizes a leaf-wise (best-first) tree growth algorithm. Leaf-wise growth splits the single leaf node that yields the largest loss reduction, regardless of depth. This allows LightGBM to grow deep, highly asymmetrical trees that can isolate tiny, high-density pockets of minority-class (mule) accounts. By setting \texttt{min\_child\_samples = 8} (compared to XGBoost's minimum child weight requirement), LightGBM is structurally configured to learn complex, minor patterns, resulting in superior sensitivity (recall).
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Inference-Time Decision Thresholds ($\tau$):}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item For default operations, the XGBoost engine uses a standard decision threshold of $\tau_{\text{xgb}} = 0.50$, prioritizing high-confidence predictions to eliminate false alarms (achieving $100.0\%$ Precision on the holdout split).
        \item During active threat waves, the LightGBM engine is paired with a dynamically lowered threshold of $\tau_{\text{lgb}} = 0.20$. Because Leaf-wise trees isolate minority cases with higher-probability leaves, lowering the threshold allows the bank to aggressively capture borderline anomalies, pushing the operational recall to $93.75\%$ (TP: 15, FN: 1) on the holdout split.
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Addressing Recall Constraints in AML}
A baseline recall of 81.25\% leaves 3 out of 16 money mules undetected in the holdout validation set, which represents an unacceptable compliance risk in live banking environments. MTX mitigates this risk by introducing a \textbf{Bimodal Toggle and Dynamic Risk Slider} in the user interface. 

During active fraud threat waves, compliance officers can lower the classification threshold ($\tau$) on the LightGBM engine to 0.20. This configuration increases the operational recall to \textbf{93.75\%} (TP: 15, FN: 1) on the holdout split, ensuring that 15 out of 16 mules are successfully intercepted. This provides banks with the flexibility to dynamically adjust their risk-appetite and operational review capacity to match current threat profiles.

\section{Sandbox Scorecard Override Rules}
\begin{equation}
P_{\text{sandbox}} = \text{clip}\left(P_{\text{model}} + \sum_{k \in \mathcal{K}} \Delta_k \cdot \mathbb{I}(\text{perturbed}_k), 0.01, 0.99\right)
\end{equation}
where $P_{\text{model}} $ is the raw ML prediction probability, and $\Delta_k$ is the override weight corresponding to the perturbed feature $k \in \mathcal{K}$ (e.g. $+35.0\%$ risk escalation for active Fraud Link $F670$, $+25.0\%$ for device switches $F2678$ or high-risk transfers $F1692$, and mitigations like $-25.0\%$ for salaried occupation $F3891$ or $-30.0\%$ for average balances below $\$100,000$ $F3811$). The indicator function $\mathbb{I}(\text{perturbed}_k)$ evaluates to 1 if feature $k$ has been manually modified in the sandbox relative to its baseline value, and 0 otherwise.

\section{Entity Resolution \& Network Graph}
To identify laundering syndicates, MTX deploys a \textbf{three-dimensional (3D) entity resolution linkage model}. While simple co-occurrence heuristics (such as matching bank branches or occupations) can serve as a starting point, they suffer from high false-positive link rates in dense regions (e.g., thousands of legitimate students registering at the same major metropolitan branch). 

To ensure the graph is defensible as a compliance-grade AML tool, MTX resolves entities by matching across three concurrent dimensions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Bank Branch Code ($F3887$):} Restricts connections to localized cohorts.
    \item \textbf{Registered Occupation ($F3891$):} Filters profiles to shared demographic subsets.
    \item \textbf{Shared Device Fingerprint (\texttt{device\_fingerprint}):} Captures literal hardware sharing (e.g., sharing a physical mobile device or overlapping IP clusters). This represents a concrete indicator of syndicate coordination.
\end{enumerate}

To simulate this network without altering the underlying dataset structure, a deterministic device fingerprint is generated on startup for each account. The fingerprint is computed as a cryptographic hash of the branch code, occupation, and a deterministic sub-cluster index ($0$, $1$, or $2$) derived from the account ID:
\begin{equation}
\text{Cluster Index} = \text{Hash}(\text{Account ID}) \pmod 3
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\text{Device Fingerprint} = \text{MD5}(\text{Branch} \parallel \text{Occupation} \parallel \text{Cluster Index})
\end{equation}
This formulation segments accounts into deterministic device-sharing sub-clusters. Nodes are linked in the vis.js network visualization if and only if they match on all three dimensions, mapping localized, device-sharing mule networks with high precision.

Nodes are colored based on evaluated threat (Red for flagged, Orange for warning, Green for safe, Purple Diamond for sandbox). Selecting a node triggers \textbf{Focus Mode}, which dims unrelated nodes to 15\% opacity to highlight the threat neighborhood.

\section{Forensic XAI \& Natural Language Generation}

\subsection{Local Feature Attribution (LFA) vs. SHAP}
To explain model decisions, we compute Local Feature Attribution (LFA) relative to clean baseline medians:
\begin{equation}
\text{Attribution}_i = (X_i - \mu_i) \times \text{Importance}_i
\end{equation}
While standard SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values provide game-theoretic guarantees (such as efficiency and symmetry), computing exact TreeSHAP values for ensemble models on a 170-dimensional space introduces an average latency of 50ms to 200ms per transaction. In retail banking, the transactional SLA requires sub-2ms latency for real-time transaction scoring. 

Our Local Feature Attribution (LFA) acts as a linear-time $O(F)$ approximation of the local explanation space. It executes in under 0.05ms, satisfying the real-time SLA while still delivering clear directionality (mitigating factors vs. risk drivers) to compliance investigators. Furthermore, LFA scales linearly with feature size, making it suitable for low-latency, high-throughput financial infrastructure.

\subsection{Natural Language Generation (NLG)}
The NLG compiler maps these values to compliance-friendly labels (e.g. ``Customer Age'' instead of $F3894$) and generates a synthesized forensic summary paragraph explaining the risk anomalies. This is automatically compiled into an exportable regulatory SAR template.

\section{REST API Reference}
The FastAPI backend serves the dashboard and exposes 7 endpoints:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\texttt{GET /}}: Serves the interactive dashboard interface.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{GET /status}}: Returns operational state, loaded models, and feature counts.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{GET /metrics}}: Returns trained model performance metrics.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{GET /metadata}}: Retrieves hybrid features list, medians, and categorical maps.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{GET /accounts}}: Retrieves pre-compiled sandbox accounts database.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{GET /simulate}}: Transaction streamer simulator returning a randomly perturbed profile.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{POST /predict}}: Main evaluation endpoint. Ingests profiles, checks for sandbox overrides, and returns probabilities, LFA scores, and NLG narratives.
\end{itemize}
